Introduction back to ToC

Activities in construction and renovation projects are related to a variety of different kinds of entities: building objects, information entities, resources, and the environment. These relations have been identified in the area of lean construction (Koskela, 1999, 2000), (Garcia-Lopetz, 2017). In the following figure different categories of flows are presented:

Each activity has a number of flows but no necessarily in all the categories shown in the figure. For instance, in the design stage the activity-flows are mostly information entities and labor - equipment, spaces, materials, or environmental conditions do not have relevance to office activities. Moreover, there can be multiple flows of each type. An activity may require multiple different equipment, labor crews, materials or information entities for their execution.

To model the logic of workflows, it is not sufficient merely to know which ingredients they have. Activities are transformations that are chained one after another based on the state changes they create. For instance, smoothing of a wall is an activity that transforms the wall surface from rough to even, and painting of the wall is an activity that requires that the surface is even. This creates a precedence constraint between the smoothing and painting activities. To make it possible to represent the transformation logic of construction processes, there is a need to represent the

  1. conditions that an activity requires to hold for it to be executed; they can be
    1. preconditions: conditions that should hold at the start of the activity
    2. execution conditions: conditions that should hold all over the execution
    >
  2. effects that the execution of the activity brings to hold

The figure above shows examples of all of these type of relations. All flows have associated conditions - both preconditions such as proper location and execution conditions such as reservation - and one flow has an effect that activity produces.

From the perspective of workflow management, activities have many properties - such as different kinds of resources and execution times - that are gradually specified in the planning and scheduling process. The possible values are, however, restricted by a set of constraints. The constraints can restrict the mutual values of different flows, or the conditions and effects of activities, as shown in the following figure:

The figure shows that the activity transport1 is of type Transport and have relation occupies interval with a time:Interval. The activity has a set of variable properties: hasActor, hasObject, hasEquipment, hasOrigin, and hasTarget. The time interval has two variable properties: hasBeginning and hasEnd. The possible values of these variables are restricted by a set of constraints. The actors are restricted to those with the capability of driving (CapabiityRequirement constraint), the vehicle is restricted to one that has sufficient load carrying capacity (LoadCarryingCapacity constraint), and the origin and target of transportation have to be different locations (DifferentValue constraint). The mutual values of beginning and end of the interval are also restricted by a MinTemporalDistance constraint, based on the duration to drive from origin to target. The activity transport1 is also related to a number of StateConstraint. They have been organized in the figure into three different groups: the conditions that need to hold at the start of the activity, the conditions that need to hold during its exexution, and the effects that will hold in the end of the activity.
